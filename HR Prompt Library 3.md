**Concise Prompt Library: Employee Relations & Conflict Resolution**

**1\. Warning for Chronic Lateness (Email)**

You are an HR manager. Write a short, professional warning email to \[Employee Name\] for repeated lateness. Include:    
\- 3–5 specific dates and minutes late    
\- Reference to company punctuality policy    
\- Impact on team    
\- Clear improvement expectation (arrive by start time)    
\- Offer to discuss any issues    
Tone: firm but empathetic, 120–180 words max. End with next steps if no improvement.

**2\. First Verbal Warning Script (In-Person/Call)**

Write a 2-minute script for a verbal warning to \[Employee Name\] about \[issue, e.g., excessive social media use during work hours\]. Structure:  

1\. State purpose  

2\. Give 2–3 specific examples  

3\. Explain policy and impact  

4\. Ask for their perspective  

5\. Agree on immediate improvement  

6\. Document that this is a verbal warning  

Tone: calm, direct, supportive.

**3\. Mediation Meeting Agenda (Two Employees in Conflict)**

Create a 45-minute mediation agenda between \[Employee A\] and \[Employee B\] regarding \[brief conflict description\]. Include:  

\- Opening ground rules  

\- Each person shares their view uninterrupted (3 min each)  

\- HR restates key points  

\- Joint problem-solving section  

\- Written agreement \+ follow-up date  

Keep neutral and solution-focused.

**4\. Response to Resignation (Keep Door Open)**

Draft a warm, 4–5 sentence resignation acceptance email to \[Employee Name\] who is leaving on \[date\]. Thank them for their contributions, wish them success, remind them about exit interview and handover, and say the door is open for future opportunities.

**5\. Handling an Anonymous Complaint**

As HR, draft a neutral company-wide email acknowledging an anonymous complaint about \[topic, e.g., favoritism in shift allocation\]. State that investigation is underway, reaffirm zero-tolerance policy, invite anyone with information to speak confidentially, and commit to fair resolution. Tone: transparent and reassuring, under 120 words.

**6\. Cooling-Off Email After Heated Argument**

Write a short same-day email to both \[Employee A\] and \[Employee B\] after a public argument. Remind them of respectful communication policy, schedule individual check-ins tomorrow, and ask them to avoid discussing the incident further until meetings occur. Tone: calm, non-accusatory.

**7\. Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) Launch Email**

Write a concise PIP notification email to \[Employee Name\]. State the meeting date to discuss the plan, list 2–3 key performance gaps with examples, expected standards, 30/60/90-day timeline, and available support. Tone: factual and supportive.

**8\. Responding to Harassment Complaint (Initial Acknowledgment)**

Draft an immediate response email to \[Employee Name\] who reported harassment by \[Alleged Person\]. Confirm receipt, assure confidentiality and non-retaliation, outline next steps (investigation within 5 working days), and provide support resources. Tone: empathetic, serious, procedural.

**9\. Return-to-Work Meeting After Conflict-Related Suspension**

Script a 10-minute return-to-work conversation with \[Employee Name\] after a 3-day suspension for \[behavior\]. Cover: acknowledgment of incident, expectations moving forward, signed commitment to policy, monitoring period, and offer of EAP support.

**10\. “We Received Feedback” Email (No Names Revealed)**

Write a short team email: “We’ve received feedback about \[general issue, e.g., loud phone calls in open areas\]. Please \[specific request\]. Thank you for keeping our workplace respectful.” Keep it positive and blame-free.
